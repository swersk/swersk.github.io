Why you Should Eat in Season

<p>Quick question: When is the best time to buy broccoli? July or December? Yeah, I didn't know either. Why does it matter though?</p><p><p style="text-align:center;">**</p></p><p>Thanksgiving dinner was coming to a close, and a huge fruit platter sat on the buffet table for dessert. As everyone grabbed their fruit of choice, my boyfriend whispered in my ear, "Watermelon? In November?" Of course, being from Italy, he only eats watermelon in July and August- when they're in season. I hadn't even thought twice about eating watermelon in November.</p><p></p><p>We're so used to any kind of food we want, whenever we want it, often overlooking the value of eating foods in in season. To clarify, when a food is in season, it means <em>that's when it's naturally grown from the Earth</em>. An orange doesn't grow in summer, so we shouldn't eat it then, for example. Watermelon grows in summer, so we should eat it then and only then.</p><p></p><p>Here are some reasons why you should eat in season:</p><p></p><p>1) <strong>Cost</strong>. The relative abundance of the crop usually lowers the price.</p><p></p><p>2) <strong>Taste</strong>. Ever had a tomato that was mealy and granular? Gross. It probably wasn't in season, shipped in from afar, or grown in unnatural conditions. When a food is not in season locally, it is grown in a hothouse or shipped from other parts of the world, both affecting the taste. When a food is transported from another part of the world, they have to be harvested early and refrigerated during the trip so they don't rot, and therefore don't ripen as well as in their natural environment. They don't develop their full flavor.</p><p></p><p>3) <b></b><strong>Save Nutrients</strong>. A food that has endured the shipping process loses nutrients along the way. Buying something in season and locally ensures it has all its nutrients.</p><p></p><p>4) <strong>Sacrifice: </strong>Have no fear, there's always something in season!
